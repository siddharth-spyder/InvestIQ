Cash: 2020 Stock Market Crash

In March 2020, COVID-19 caused one of the fastest stock market crashes in history. The S&P 500 dropped 34% in just 23 days. Investors with cash were able to pay their bills during lockdowns and even buy stocks at discount prices. Cash provided stability when everything else was uncertain.

Bonds: 2008 Financial Crisis

During the 2008 financial crisis, the stock market lost more than 50% of its value. However, safe government bonds actually went up as investors fled to safety. People who had a mix of stocks and bonds in their portfolio experienced much smaller losses than those who only owned stocks.

Index Funds: Long-Term S&P 500 Growth

Since 1926, the S&P 500 has returned an average of about 10% per year, despite multiple crashes and recessions. Investors who bought index funds tracking the S&P 500 and held for 20+ years almost always made money. The key was patience through the ups and downs.

Stocks: Amazon & The Dot-com Crash

Amazon stock fell from $107 to $7 (a 95% drop) during the 2000-2002 dot-com crash. Many investors sold in panic. But those who held on saw Amazon become one of the most valuable companies in the world. However, most other dot-com companies never recovered and went to zero.

Crypto: Bitcoin's Wild Ride

Bitcoin has experienced several boom-and-bust cycles. In 2017, it rose from $1,000 to $20,000, then crashed to $3,000 by 2018. In 2021, it hit $69,000, only to fall to $16,000 in 2022. This extreme volatility is why experts recommend keeping crypto to a small portion of your portfolio.

Diversification: The 60/40 Portfolio

For decades, financial advisors recommended a "60/40" portfolio: 60% stocks and 40% bonds. This mix provided growth from stocks while bonds cushioned the falls during crashes. While it's not perfect, diversification has historically reduced risk without sacrificing too much return.
